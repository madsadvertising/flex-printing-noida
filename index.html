Why this exists

One material, a hundred different jobs

Walk down almost any commercial stretch in Noida or Greater Noida and the skyline is stitched together with flex — shop boards, real-estate banners, election hoardings, glowing pharmacy signs above shuttered shops at midnight. It's the default material for outdoor advertising in India because a square foot of decent quality costs about as much as a cup of chai, yet it shrugs off a Delhi summer and a Noida monsoon without much fuss.

The catch is that "flex printing" isn't one product with one price. The same word covers a flimsy banner meant to survive one weekend sale and a five-year, weatherproof board outside a showroom — and the gap in price between the two is most of what this guide is trying to make clear.

The three you'll actually be quoted

Frontlit, backlit, star flex

Daylight / ambient light

Frontlit

The default flex. An opaque white backing printed on the face, meant to be read in sunlight or under a spotlight — this is what's behind roughly nine out of ten shop boards, hoardings and event banners around NCR.

Best for shop signage, hoardings, banners, standees. Ask for 280–340 GSM if it's staying up longer than a season.

Illuminated from behind

Backlit

A translucent base built to glow evenly when lit from inside a signbox — pharmacy signs, glow boards above a shop entrance, light-box displays. Using ordinary frontlit material here gives a patchy, uneven glow instead of a clean one.

Best for glow sign boards, light boxes, night-visible branding. Typically 30–50% pricier than frontlit because of the specialised base material.

Premium / long-term

Star flex

A denser flex with a smoother finish, aimed at anything that needs to still look sharp in year three, not just month three — builder hoardings, showroom fronts, long-running highway boards. Higher GSM, better colour hold, far less sagging.

Best for long-term outdoor display, premium branding. Typical life 3–5 years vs 1–3 for standard frontlit.

Two more terms you'll hear: Black-back / blockout flex has an opaque black middle layer, used when a banner faces direct sun from behind so the printed image doesn't wash out. And eco-solvent vs latex vs UV describes the ink and printing process rather than the flex sheet itself — eco-solvent is the budget default, latex prints a bit cleaner and dries faster, and UV-cured ink costs the most but holds colour best in direct sun.

Before you place the order

How to get a print that doesn't disappoint

  • Check the GSM, not just the type. A 280 GSM frontlit and a 510 GSM star flex are both "flex" — the heavier one is what survives wind and rain for years instead of months.
  • Ask which ink process they're using. Eco-solvent is the economical default; latex and UV cost more but resist fading and look sharper up close.
  • Match resolution to viewing distance. A close-range shop board needs higher DPI than a highway hoarding seen from 50 metres — don't overpay for resolution nobody will notice, or underpay for one people will.
  • Request a physical sample swatch before committing to a large run, especially for backlit or star flex where the material itself is the main cost driver.
  • Confirm the finishing details: eyelet spacing, hemmed edges, and frame material (MS, iron, or wood) if it's being mounted.
  • For backlit jobs, double-check the material is genuinely translucent backlit stock — not a frontlit print lit from behind, which glows unevenly and looks patchy at night.
  • Send print-ready artwork at full size, ideally 100–150 DPI minimum at actual dimensions, in CMYK — blurry banners are usually a file problem, not a printer problem.

Choosing who to call

Finding a printer worth trusting

01

Shortlist by rating, not just rank

Search "flex printing near me" on Google Maps, JustDial or IndiaMART and sort by rating with a healthy number of reviews — a 4.8 with 3 reviews tells you less than a 4.4 with 200.

02

Ask for aged samples

A printer's freshest sample always looks great. Ask to see something that's been outdoors for a year — that's the print quality you're actually paying for.

03

Match machine to job

Large-format eco-solvent, latex, and UV flatbed machines all produce different results. A vendor running the right machine for your run length and finish will usually say so without being asked twice.

04

Get an itemised quote

Per-sq-ft rate, GSM, ink type, finishing, and delivery date — in writing. Vague verbal quotes are where most disputes start.

05

Check if installation is included

Frame mounting and on-site installation are often billed separately. Ask up front, not after the banner arrives.

06

Compare at least three quotes

Price gaps between vendors usually trace back to GSM and ink quality, not margin alone — three quotes make that visible.
A reasonable rule of thumb: any vendor happy to show you a one-year-old outdoor sample and break their quote down by GSM and ink type is one worth ordering from.

Quick answers

Frequently asked

What's the cheapest flex printing option?

Standard eco-solvent frontlit at a lower GSM (around 280) is the budget default — fine for short campaigns of a few weeks to a few months, less suited to anything left up year-round.

How long does flex printing actually last outdoors?

Standard frontlit typically holds up 1–3 years depending on GSM and ink. Premium star flex with UV ink can stretch to 3–5 years. Direct coastal-style sun, heavy pollution, or storm exposure shortens all of these by roughly a third.

What's the real difference between frontlit and backlit?

Frontlit is opaque and meant to be viewed under ambient or external light — sun, streetlight, a spotlight. Backlit is translucent and designed to be lit from inside a signbox so it glows evenly at night. Using one in place of the other gives noticeably worse results.

Can a flex banner be reused or reprinted?

Not really reprinted — but if the content stays the same and the eyelets are intact, a well-made banner can be rehung for a second event. For frequent reuse, vinyl or fabric banners hold up better than flex.
